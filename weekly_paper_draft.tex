\section{Transaction Building Discussion - Overcoming Spatial Overlap Bias}

\subsection{The Problem: Overlapping Transactions in Standard Algorithms}
In standard, unconstrained association rule mining, defining transactions as patches around every cell creates severe spatial overlap. In dense regions, an interaction between Cell A and Cell B is redundantly captured by the overlapping patches of many surrounding bystander cells. This naive approach artificially inflates the Joint Support and generates false positive associations.

\subsection{The Solution: Role-Restricted Rules}
To prevent double-counting, each patch is assigned exactly one \texttt{CENTER} and multiple \texttt{NEIGHBOR}s. We constrain the algorithm to only mine directional rules:
\begin{equation}
    A\_\text{CENTER} \rightarrow B\_\text{NEIGHBOR}
\end{equation}

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Note (Weighted variant):} \textit{In the \textbf{binary pipeline}, a \texttt{NEIGHBOR} item is 1 if any cell of that type exists within radius $r$, and 0 otherwise. In the \textbf{weighted pipeline}, each neighbor contributes a Gaussian-decayed weight $w = \exp\!\bigl(-\tfrac{d^2}{2b^2}\bigr) \in [0,1]$; weights from multiple cells of the same type accumulate and are capped at 1.0. The proof below is stated in binary terms and holds equally for the weighted case.}
\end{quote}

\subsection*{Mathematical Proof of Unbiased Confidence}
Let $N$ be the number of \textbf{valid patches} (cells whose neighborhood passes the minimum-size and dominance filters). Because the rule strictly requires cell $A$ to be the \texttt{CENTER}, the FP-Growth metrics behave as follows:

\paragraph{1. Antecedent Support:}
Counts the proportion of valid patches where A is the exact center:
\begin{equation}
    Support(A\_\text{CENTER}) = \frac{\text{A cells in valid patches}}{N}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{2. Joint Support:}
Counts the proportion of patches centered on A that also contain B. Bystander patches are ignored because their center is not A:
\begin{equation}
    Support(A\_\text{CENTER} \cap B\_\text{NEIGHBOR}) = \frac{\text{A cells with a B neighbour}}{N}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{3. Confidence (The Unbiased Metric):}
Confidence is mathematically defined as the ratio of Joint Support to Antecedent Support. $N$ cancels out:
\begin{equation}
    Confidence = \frac{Support(A\_\text{CENTER} \cap B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})}{Support(A\_\text{CENTER})} = \frac{\text{A cells with a B neighbour}}{\text{A cells in valid patches}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Concrete Example:}
Consider a tissue with $N = 100$ valid patches, of which 10 are centred on $A$ cells. Exactly 4 of these $A$ cells have a $B$ cell in their neighbourhood.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $Support(A\_\text{CENTER}) = \frac{10}{100} = 0.10$
    \item $Support(A\_\text{CENTER} \cap B\_\text{NEIGHBOR}) = \frac{4}{100} = 0.04$
    \item $Confidence = \frac{0.04}{0.10} = \frac{4}{10} = 0.40$ (exactly 40\% of $A$ cells have a $B$ neighbour).
\end{itemize}
Because $N$ cancels out, the calculation discards the redundant bystander patches, leaving a pure, unbiased ratio of spatial colocation.

\subsection*{The Effect of Spatial Density on Neighborhood Support}
When calculating the $Support(\text{Consequent}\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$, the algorithm caps the weight per patch at a maximum of 1.0. Because of this cap, dense, clustered cells "waste" their absolute cell count. Tightly packed cells hit the 1.0 limit immediately, resulting in a small spatial footprint. In contrast, the same number of cells spread evenly across the tissue trigger the 1.0 score in many different patches, creating a much larger footprint.

Figure \ref{fig:demo_clustered_dispersed} empirically proves this. In \textit{Control\_01\_FOV\_3}, clustered Muscle cells and dispersed CD8T cells have identical absolute counts ($\sim130$ cells, $\sim6\%$ of the FOV). However, due to density, the Muscle footprint covers only 13.3\% of the tissue, while the dispersed CD8T footprint covers 50.2\%.

\subsection*{The Geometric Baseline: What Lift Measures}
Lift normalizes Confidence by $B$'s overall tissue accessibility:
\begin{equation}
    Lift = \frac{Confidence}{Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})}
\end{equation}

$Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$ is the fraction of valid patches that contain at least one $B$ cell within radius $r$. This is entirely determined by $B$'s \textbf{spatial footprint} — the fraction of tissue area covered by a radius-$r$ disc around any $B$ cell. Crucially, in binary, \textbf{the count of $B$ cells per patch does not affect Support} — only presence matters. Two options:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dispersed $B$} (cells spread throughout tissue) $\Rightarrow$ large footprint $\Rightarrow$ high $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$ $\Rightarrow$ Lift suppressed toward 1 (Fig~\ref{fig:demo_dispersed}).
    \item \textbf{Spatially restricted $B$} (cells confined to a small region) $\Rightarrow$ small footprint $\Rightarrow$ low $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$ $\Rightarrow$ high Lift (Fig \ref{fig:demo_dense}).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Implication:} High Lift signals that $B$ is spatially rare - not that the association is stronger than a rule with moderate Lift. When $B$ is dispersed, Confidence is the more diagnostic metric. Fig~\ref{fig:demo_dispersed} illustrates the concept.

\subsection*{Metric Verification: Visualization}
Each rule can be inspected with the 8-panel verification plot (Figures~\ref{fig:demo_dispersed}--\ref{fig:demo_complex}; notebook \texttt{investigation\_11}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(A)} Full FOV — spatial context.
    \item \textbf{(B)} Absolute antecedent cells — raw locations of all $A$ cells.
    \item \textbf{(C)} Antecedent occurrences — patches where the full antecedent fires. For complex antecedents, a strict spatial subset of (B). For single antecedent - same as (B).
    \item \textbf{(D)} Missed antecedents — antecedent fired but consequent did not (the Confidence ``misses'').
    \item \textbf{(E)} Absolute consequent cells — raw locations of all $B$ cells.
    \item \textbf{(F)} \textbf{Consequent occurrences} — patches containing $B$ as a neighbor. \textbf{This is $B$'s spatial footprint}; its area directly sets $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$.
    \item \textbf{(G)} Joint occurrences — patches where both antecedent and consequent fire (numerator of Confidence).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Complex antecedents and data fragmentation.}
The architecture extends naturally to multi-item antecedents:
\begin{equation}
    A\_\text{CENTER},\;B\_\text{NEIGHBOR} \rightarrow C\_\text{NEIGHBOR}
\end{equation}
$N$ still cancels, and Confidence becomes the fraction of A cells that have \emph{both} B and C neighbors, among those that already have B:
\begin{equation}
    Confidence = \frac{\text{A cells with both B and C neighbors}}{\text{A cells with a B neighbor}}
\end{equation}
In the verification plot, Panel C (antecedent occurrences) is now a strict spatial subset of Panel B (all absolute antecedent cells): only A cells that already satisfy the full antecedent condition appear in C, and C is the Confidence denominator. As the antecedent grows, Panel C shrinks — hence the need for strict minimum support thresholds.
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Notes}\label{sec:notes}

\paragraph{1. Small absolute count}
\texttt{MIN\_ABS\_SUPPORT} sets a transaction floor but does not prevent spuriously high metrics when a cell type is very rare in a FOV. With only $\sim$10 A cells, Confidence $= 1.0$ and extreme Lift are trivially achievable by chance. \texttt{Rule\_Count\_Global} (the number of FOVs that independently carry the rule) is the key robustness indicator (Fig \ref{fig:demo_smallcount}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Weekly Summary #3 (07.06.2026)/demo 3 endocrine to eplithelial.png}

    \caption{\textbf{Small count.} Rule \textbf{Endocrine $\to$ Epithelial}, \textit{GVHD\_08\_FOV\_2}. \textbf{(B) and (C)} (in this case they are the same because there's only a single antecedent) shows only a handful of Endocrine cells. Confidence $= 1.0$; Lift $= 11.25$ — but the rule appears in only 5 FOVs globally.}
    \label{fig:demo_smallcount}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{2. Dense consequent throughout the tissue: Lift becomes uninformative or misleading.}
When $B$ is globally abundant, $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$ is very high — most patches in the FOV have $B$ as a neighbor regardless of their center cell type. This suppresses Lift even for strong positive associations: if Confidence $<$ $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$, Lift drops \emph{below 1}, making the rule appear as a negative/avoidance pattern when it is actually genuine co-localization. \textbf{When $B$ is globally dense, use Confidence as the primary metric — Lift is not interpretable} (Fig \ref{fig:demo_dense_negative}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Weekly Summary #3 (07.06.2026)/confidence high but negative lift.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Dense consequent, Lift $< 1$ despite genuine association.} Rule \textbf{Plasma $\to$ Epithelial}, \textit{Control\_06\_FOV\_2}. \textbf{(B)}: only 21 Plasma cells (1.3\% of FOV). \textbf{(E/F)}: Epithelial cells blanket 98.1\% of the tissue. Confidence $= 0.674$ — 67\% of Plasma patches genuinely have an Epithelial neighbor. Lift $= 0.687 < 1$ — the rule appears as avoidance, because achieving Lift $\geq 1$ would require Confidence $> 0.981$. Plasma does not avoid Epithelial: Lift is uninformative when B is this globally dense.}
    \label{fig:demo_dense_negative}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{3. Edge neighborhood truncation.}
Cells near the FOV boundary have truncated neighborhoods - the radius-$r$ disc extends partly outside the imaged area. Both the joint-support numerator and the antecedent-support denominator shrink by the same factor, so \textbf{Confidence is approximately preserved}. However, $Support(B\_\text{NEIGHBOR})$ from edge patches is suppressed, which \textbf{inflates Lift} for rules where $A$ preferentially occupies tissue boundaries. The permutation test is unaffected (it preserves physical positions), so FDR is valid - but Lift values for boundary-associated $A$ types should not be compared directly with those from interior-associated types (Fig \ref{fig:demo_edge}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Weekly Summary #3 (07.06.2026)/demo 5 edge neighborhoods.png}
    \caption{\textbf{Edge truncation.} Rule \textbf{Muscle $\to$ Neuron}, \textit{GVHD\_54\_FOV\_2}. \textbf({B)}: Muscle cells sit at the sub-mucosal boundary. Their radius-$r$ neighbourhoods are truncated half-discs — fewer Neurons are accessible from these edge patches than from equivalent interior positions, suppressing $Support(\text{Neuron\_NEIGHBOR}) = 0.279$ and inflating Lift. Confidence $= 0.87$ (robust to truncation); Lift $= 3.10$ (not directly comparable to Lift from interior-associated rules).}

\section{Proposed Threshold Framework}

We propose a multi-metric framework to filter spatial noise and identify relevant patterns based on tissue context.

\subsection{Simple Rules (One-to-One): Addressing Density Artifacts}
High global density of consequent cell ($B$) suppresses Lift toward 1.0 or below, mislabeling co-localization as avoidance. We resolve this by pivoting metrics based on prevalence.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Context-Aware Gating:} 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Sparse Consequent ($Supp(B) < 0.5$):} Use \textbf{Lift $>$ 2.0}.
        \item \textbf{Dense Consequent ($Supp(B) \geq 0.5$):} Use \textbf{Conviction $>$ 1.2} and \textbf{Confidence $>$ 0.8}. 
        \item \textit{Why:} Conviction identifies dependencies by measuring the reduction in ``misses'' ($A$ without $B$), remaining sensitive when high density breaks Lift.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rare Cell Floor:} 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Threshold:} \textbf{Antecedent Count $\geq 10$}.
        \item \textit{Why:} Prevents accidental alignments of rare cells from generating fragile metrics.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Complex Rules (3+ items): Addressing Redundancy and Stability}
Complex rules suffer from denominator fragmentation and redundancy over simpler subsets.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Redundancy Filter (Occam's razor):} 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Threshold:} \textbf{Confidence Ratio $>$ 1.1}.
        \item \textit{Why:} Discards complex rules unless the combination is at least 10\% more predictive than its best simple subset.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stability Gate (Antecedent Support):} 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Threshold:} \textbf{Antecedent Support $>$ 0.05} (scaled by length).
        \item \textit{Why:} Gating the denominator (the conditional subset) ensures Confidence is calculated from a robust sample.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
