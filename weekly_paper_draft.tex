\subsection*{1.2 The Weighted FP-Growth Math}
Standard association rule mining treats cell co-occurrence as binary. To capture the continuous nature of biological diffusion, our pipeline uses a Weighted FP-Growth approach.

\textbf{1. Building the Transaction:} 
For every center cell, we define a neighborhood using a fixed radius $R$. Instead of a binary list, we assign each neighbor a continuous weight $w_i$ based on its distance $d_i$ from the center, using a Gaussian decay function with bandwidth $\sigma$:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \exp\left(-\frac{d_i^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
    \label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
A single transaction is therefore a dictionary of cell-weights (e.g., \textit{[Macrophage-Center: 1.0, Epithelial-Neighbor: 0.9, CD8T-Neighbor: 0.1]}).

\textbf{2. Calculating the Metrics:} 
In standard binary data, finding cells that co-occur is identical to multiplication ($1 \times 1 = 1$). However, with continuous weights, we must use the $\min()$ function (the "fuzzy AND" operator). This is crucial to preserve the \textbf{Anti-Monotone Property} (the Apriori principle \cite{ref_apriori}), which guarantees that the support of a combined rule ($A \cup B$) can never exceed the support of its individual components.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Support ($\mathcal{S}$):} For a specific rule, we take the lowest weight among the items \textit{involved in that rule}, and average this value across all $N$ neighborhoods in the FOV:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{S}(A \cup B) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \min_{i \in A \cup B} (w_{i,j})
        \label{eq:support}
    \end{equation}
    \textit{Intuition:} If we evaluate $A \rightarrow B$, where A is at the center ($w=1.0$) and B is at the edge of the radius ($w=0.1$), the score is $\min(1.0, 0.1) = 0.1$. The cell furthest away acts as a bottleneck, ensuring the rule only achieves high support if \textit{all} cells in the rule are physically close together.
    \item \textbf{Confidence ($\frac{\mathcal{S}(A \cup B)}{\mathcal{S}(A)}$):} Measures weight matching. It asks: "When A is strongly present, how strong is B?"
    \item \textbf{Lift ($\frac{\text{Confidence}}{\mathcal{S}(B)}$):} Measures co-diffusion over random chance. A Lift $> 1$ means A and B cluster together more closely than expected independently.
    \item \textbf{Conviction ($\frac{1 - \mathcal{S}(B)}{1 - \text{Confidence}}$):} Measures how often A occurs \textit{without} B. In our continuous space, a high conviction means finding A with a high weight while B has a low weight is extremely rare.
\end{itemize}

\section*{2. The Week's Narrative}

\subsection*{2.1 Open Issues (Entering the Week)}
We entered the week with two overarching problems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Alignment Failure:} Our mined results do not match the insights from the spatial atlas reference paper \cite{ref_gvhd}. Specifically, we expect to see healthy homeostasis (Paneth $\rightarrow$ Epi $\downarrow$), barrier loss (Plasma $\rightarrow$ Epi $\downarrow$), and pathogenic swarms (CD8T\_GZMB+ $\rightarrow$ Epi $\uparrow$).
    \item \textbf{Independent Validation:} If the mismatch's caused by the data itself rather than algorithmic bias, we currently lack a robust framework to validate our results independently of the reference paper's specific findings.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{2.2 This Week's Objectives}
Our primary target this week was tackling the first issue: debugging the pipeline to determine why it fails to capture the known biological signatures.

\section*{4. Next Steps \& Questions for Next Week}

\subsection*{4.1 Refining the Pipeline}
Based on literature review, we suspect two algorithmic biases are causing the mismatch, which we will address:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rare Items:} Target immune cells are rare. We will evaluate \textbf{MISFP-Growth} \cite{ref_misfp} to assign lower support thresholds specifically to rare cells without exploding trivial background rules.
    \item \textbf{Abundance \& Overcounting:} We will test the \textbf{Participation Index} \cite{ref_participation} and \textbf{Fraction-Score} \cite{ref_fraction} to prevent dense background structural cells from dominating the continuous metric space.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{4.2 Alternative Validation Strategies}
While we refine the pipeline, we need to ensure the algorithm isn't finding \textit{different} but valid biology.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Primary Focus:} We will perform visual sanity checks on obvious FOVs by plotting the raw coordinates. We will also visualize our highest-ranked rules to see if they make sense.

     \item \textbf{Pipeline Investigation:} We will address algorithmic biases by evaluating MISFP-Growth for rare cell detection,
     alongside the Participation Index and Fraction-Score to correct abundance biases in dense tissue regions. 
    
    \item \textbf{Further Result Investigation:} Evaluate Fold Changes between stages (instead of absolute Lift variance), test alternative metrics like Conviction, and cross-reference our significant rules against other established literature.
\end{itemize}
