\subsection*{1.2 The Weighted FP-Growth Math}
Standard association rule mining treats cell co-occurrence as binary. To capture the continuous nature of biological diffusion, our pipeline uses a Weighted FP-Growth approach \cite{ref_weighted_fpgrowth}.

\textbf{1. Building the Transaction:} 
For every center cell, we define a neighborhood using a fixed radius $R$. Instead of a binary list, we assign each neighbor a continuous weight $w_i$ based on its distance $d_i$ from the center, using a Gaussian decay function with bandwidth $\sigma$:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \exp\left(-\frac{d_i^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
    \label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
A single transaction is therefore a dictionary of cell-weights (e.g. \textit{[Macrophage-Center: 1.0, Epithelial-Neighbor: 0.9, CD8T-Neighbor: 0.1]}). For each cell type in the neighborhood we sum the Gaussian weights and then \textbf{cap it} at $1.0$.

\textbf{\textit{Capping at $1.0$}} is necessary to constrain the values within standard  probability bounds ($[0,1]$), ensuring metrics like Lift and Conviction do not break from values exceeding 100\% support. 

We use this independent capping rather than fractional normalization to prevent abundant structural cells from artificially diluting the spatial signals of rare immune cells, at the cost of saturating density signals.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{2. Calculating the Metrics:} 
In standard binary data, finding cells that co-occur is identical to multiplication ($1 \times 1 = 1$). However, with continuous weights, we must use the $\min()$ function (the "fuzzy AND" operator). This is crucial to preserve the \textbf{Anti-Monotone Property} (the Apriori principle \cite{ref_weighted_fpgrowth}), which guaranties that the support of a combined rule ($A \cup B$) can never exceed the support of its individual components.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Support ($\mathcal{S}$):} For a specific rule, we take the lowest weight among the items involved in that rule, and average this value across all $N$ neighborhoods in the FOV:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{S}(A \cup B) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{N} \min_{i \in A \cup B} (w_{i,j})
        \label{eq:support}
    \end{equation}
    \textit{Intuition:} If we evaluate $A \rightarrow B$, where A is at the center ($w=1.0$) and B is at the edge of the radius ($w=0.1$), the score is $\min(1.0, 0.1) = 0.1$. The cell furthest away acts as a bottleneck, ensuring the rule only achieves high support if \textit{all} cells in the rule are physically close together.
    \item \textbf{Confidence ($\frac{\mathcal{S}(A \cup B)}{\mathcal{S}(A)}$):} Measures weight matching. It asks: "When A is strongly present, how strong is B?"
    \item \textbf{Lift ($\frac{\mathcal{S}(A \cup B)}{\mathcal{S}(A) \times \mathcal{S}(B)}$):} Measures co-diffusion over random chance. A Lift $> 1$ means A and B cluster together more closely than expected independently.
    \item \textbf{Conviction ($\frac{1 - \mathcal{S}(B)}{1 - \text{Confidence}}$):} Measures how often A occurs \textit{without} B. In our continuous space, a high conviction means finding A with a high weight while B has a low weight is extremely rare.
\end{itemize}

\section*{2. The Week's Narrative}

\subsection*{2.1 Open Issues (Entering the Week)}
We entered the week with two problems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Alignment Failure:} Our mined results do not match the insights from the spatial atlas reference paper \cite{ref_gvhd}. Specifically, we expect to see healthy homeostasis (Paneth $\rightarrow$ Epi $\downarrow$), barrier loss (Plasma $\rightarrow$ Epi $\downarrow$), and pathogenic swarms (CD8T\_GZMB+ $\rightarrow$ Epi $\uparrow$).
    \item \textbf{Independent Validation:} If the mismatch's caused by the data itself rather than algorithmic bias, we currently lack a robust framework to validate our results independently of the reference paper's specific findings.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{2.2 This Week's Objectives}
Our primary target this week was tackling the first issue: debugging the pipeline to determine why it fails to capture the known biological signatures.

\section*{4. Next Steps \& Questions for Next Weeks}
In the next weeks, we need to (1) refine the pipeline, (2) validate algorithm's results, independently of the biological paper's findings.

\subsection*{4.1 Primary Focus - Upcoming Week:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sanity Check:} We will perform visual sanity checks on obvious FOVs by plotting the raw coordinates. 
    
    \item \textbf{Rule Visualization:} We will visualize our highest-ranked rules to see if they make sense.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{4.2 Further Investigation Ideas:}

\begin{itemize}
    
    \item Alternative Validation Strategies:
    
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Result Investigation:} (a) Mine the rules for grouped FOVs per stage, instead of mining them per FOV (maybe group them not just per stage but per metadata-features?) (b) Evaluate Fold Changes between stages (instead of absolute Lift variance), (c) test alternative metrics like Conviction, and (d) cross-reference our significant rules against other established literature.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item Refining the Pipeline:
    
    Based on literature review, we suspect two algorithmic biases are causing the mismatch, which we will address:
    
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Rare Items:} Target immune cells are rare. We will evaluate \textbf{MISFP-Growth} \cite{ref_misfp} to assign lower support thresholds specifically to rare cells without exploding trivial background rules.
        \item \textbf{Abundance \& Overcounting:} We will test the \textbf{Participation Index} \cite{ref_participation} and \textbf{Fraction-Score} \cite{ref_fraction} to prevent dense background structural cells from dominating the continuous metric space.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
